\chapter{Literature Review}


\section{A Weakly Informative Default Prior Distribution for Logistic and Other Regression Models}
Gelman et al.'s paper addresses an important practical problem in logistic regression and related regression models: the instability of coefficient estimates when data are sparse, predictors are highly imbalanced, or separation occurs. In such cases, classical maximum likelihood estimation may produce very large or undefined coefficient estimates, making inference unreliable. The authors argue that fully informative priors may be difficult to specify in routine applied work, while completely noninformative priors may fail to regularise unstable likelihoods. Their contribution is therefore motivated by the need for a default Bayesian prior that is weak enough to allow the data to dominate when the data are informative, but strong enough to prevent implausibly large coefficient estimates in poorly identified logistic regression problems.

The main methodological contribution of the paper is the proposal of weakly informative Student-\(t\) prior distributions for coefficients in logistic and related regression models. In particular, the authors recommend a default Cauchy prior with centre \(0\) and scale \(2.5\) for regression coefficients after nonbinary predictors have been scaled appropriately. This paper is relevant to the present thesis because the methodology uses Bayesian logistic regression, and the choice of prior distribution affects posterior estimation before applying Metropolis--Hastings sampling and Consensus Monte Carlo on distributed data subsets. However, the paper is not primarily concerned with distributed Bayesian computation or posterior aggregation. Its role in this literature review is therefore to justify the Bayesian logistic regression and prior specification component of the methodology, rather than to provide a direct method for Consensus Monte Carlo. \cite{gelman2008weakly}

\section{Comparing Consensus Monte Carlo Strategies for Distributed Bayesian Computation}
This paper by Steven L. Scott reviews and compares methods for distributed Bayesian computation, focusing on how to combine posterior samples generated separately on different data partitions. Its main concern is the consensus Monte Carlo setting, where large datasets are split across machines to reduce computational burden and communication costs. Scott evaluates three main aggregation strategies already discussed in the literature: averaging methods, sequential resampling approaches, and density-estimation methods. The paper shows that simple weighted averaging performs very well when subposteriors are close to Gaussian, which often happens in large-sample settings, making it a strong practical baseline despite its simplicity.

A major contribution of the paper is its critical comparison of these methods and the proposal of a mixture-based consensus Monte Carlo approach, which uses Gaussian mixtures to better capture non-Gaussian posterior shapes. Scott finds that while more flexible methods such as kernel-based and sequential approaches are attractive in theory, they tend to perform poorly as dimension increases, whereas averaging remains stable and computationally efficient. The paper therefore concludes that in distributed Bayesian inference, the main challenge is not generating local posterior samples but combining them accurately without losing scalability. Overall, the study is important because it gives a balanced assessment of the trade-off between statistical accuracy and computational practicality in scalable Bayesian analysis. \cite{scott2017comparing}

\section{Bayes and Big Data: The Consensus Monte Carlo Algorithm}
Scott et al.’s Bayes and Big Data: The Consensus Monte Carlo Algorithm sits in the early literature on scalable Bayesian computation, addressing the problem of inference when data are too large for a single machine. The paper argues that existing parallel approaches based on multicore CPUs or GPUs reduce processor bottlenecks but do not solve memory and disk limits, while multi-machine settings introduce severe communication costs that make standard synchronized MCMC inefficient. In positioning their method, the authors connect their work to several adjacent strands: averaging frequentist estimators in distributed settings, the “bag of little bootstraps,” earlier Bayesian consensus ideas by Huang and Gelman, and deterministic Bayesian approximations such as INLA and variational Bayes. Their literature framing is therefore important because it shows consensus Monte Carlo not as an isolated algorithm, but as one response within a broader effort to make inference feasible under big-data constraints while still preserving access to the full posterior distribution.

The paper’s main contribution is to propose consensus Monte Carlo, where data are split into shards, each worker runs an independent Monte Carlo simulation on its local subproblem, and the resulting draws are combined through averaging rules to approximate the full posterior. In the examples, the method performs well not only in Gaussian settings but also in some non-Gaussian cases, including beta-binomial models, hierarchical models, and BART, which supports the authors’ claim that simple averaging can be surprisingly effective in distributed Bayesian analysis. At the same time, the paper is careful about limitations: the method is best suited to fixed-dimensional continuous parameter spaces, and the authors explicitly note that performance away from Gaussianity remains an open question, with more sophisticated consensus mechanisms still needing development. As a result, this paper is best read as a foundational contribution that establishes the practicality of low-communication Bayesian aggregation, while also defining the main theoretical and methodological issues that later distributed MCMC research would need to resolve.
\cite{scott2022bayes}

\section{Sampling for Bayesian computation with large datasets}
Huang and Gelman’s Sampling for Bayesian Computation with Large Datasets is an early contribution to the literature on scalable Bayesian inference for hierarchical models. The paper begins from the observation that Bayesian multilevel modeling had already become important across fields such as health, education, environment, and business, but that standard Gibbs and related MCMC methods became difficult to use once datasets were too large for convenient storage and repeated full-data iteration. In that context, the authors position their work against earlier data-subsetting approaches, especially sequential and importance-sampling based methods such as Chopin (2002) and Ridgeway and Madigan (2002), arguing that their own strategy extends this line of work by using random partitioning and cluster sampling so that each subset preserves the hierarchical structure of the original data. This framing is important because it treats big-data Bayes not as a completely new problem, but as an extension of existing work on approximate posterior computation under computational constraints.

The paper’s main methodological contribution is a consensus-style posterior combination strategy: divide the data into disjoint subsets, fit the Bayesian model separately on each subset, and then combine the resulting inferences using a normal approximation, with possible refinement through importance sampling. In the literature, this paper is therefore best understood as a precursor to later consensus Monte Carlo methods, because it already identifies the central ideas that would define that area: subset-based inference, low communication cost, and the statistical problem of how to recombine local posterior information into a global approximation. The authors are also careful to state the unresolved issues, including the lack of a full theoretical justification, the choice of the number of subsets K, and the question of which aggregation rule is best. As a result, the paper is significant less for providing a final solution than for establishing the basic research agenda for distributed Bayesian computation in large hierarchical datasets. \cite{huang2005sampling}
