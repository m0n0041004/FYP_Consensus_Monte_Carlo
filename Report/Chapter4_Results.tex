\chapter{Results and Analysis}

\section{Data Summary}

This chapter reports the empirical results for Bayesian logistic regression applied to the UK accident dataset. The binary response is accident severity, where \texttt{serious\_fatal} is coded as 1 and is the event being modelled, while \texttt{slight} is coded as 0 and is the reference response class. The predictors are \texttt{road\_type} and \texttt{speed\_limit}. The reference category for \texttt{road\_type} is \texttt{single carriageway}, and the reference category for \texttt{speed\_limit} is \texttt{2}.

Table \ref{tab:model_settings} summarises the main analysis settings. After preprocessing, the analysis contains 82,344 observations and 10 regression coefficients. The prior standard deviation for the normal prior is 2.5. Both the full-data and subset Metropolis--Hastings samplers use 100,000 iterations, with a burn-in fraction of 0.2. Consensus Monte Carlo uses four subsets of equal size.

The odds-ratio summaries show the same pattern because they are obtained by exponentiating the log-odds scale posterior summaries. Across the four methods, the 95\% odds-ratio interval for \texttt{road\_typeone way street (from 2005)} includes 1, while the corresponding intervals for the other non-intercept coefficients do not include 1. The effective sample size and Monte Carlo standard error outputs indicate that the full-data Independent Metropolis--Hastings sampler has larger effective sample sizes and smaller Monte Carlo standard errors than the full-data Random Walk Metropolis--Hastings sampler. The CMC-IMH effective sample size values are also large in the exported summaries. These values are not interpreted alone, because the Consensus Monte Carlo density plots are based on combined consensus samples rather than a single Markov chain, and diagnostic plots, acceptance rates, and runtime should be considered together.

\section{Summary of Findings}

The empirical results show that the full-data Random Walk Metropolis--Hastings and full-data Independent Metropolis--Hastings samplers produce very similar posterior summaries for the Bayesian logistic regression model. This agreement provides a baseline for evaluating the Consensus Monte Carlo approximations.

The Consensus Monte Carlo posterior summaries are generally close to the full-data posterior summaries. The CMC-RWMH and CMC-IMH posterior means differ most visibly from the full-data estimates for \texttt{road\_typeslip road (from 2005)} and \texttt{speed\_limit1}, but the overall posterior summaries remain similar across methods. The credible intervals and odds-ratio intervals also show broadly consistent conclusions across the four methods.

The acceptance-rate and runtime results should be interpreted descriptively. In the current sequential implementation, full-data IMH has the shortest recorded runtime, while CMC-RWMH and CMC-IMH do not demonstrate parallel speedup. Overall, the results suggest that Consensus Monte Carlo gives posterior summaries close to the full-data posterior summaries in this empirical analysis, but this conclusion is based on the reported posterior summaries, diagnostics, acceptance rates, and sequential runtime values rather than on an assumed computational advantage.
